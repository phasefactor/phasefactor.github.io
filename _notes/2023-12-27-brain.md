These supplements have good evidence supporting their usefulness in long-term brain health and in the prevention of cognitive decline.  

Some promote better performance on cognitive tests in the short term, but that was not the primary selector for supplements on this list.

## Actionable Takeaways
The following are recommended for supplementation.

| Type | Recommended Supplement
| --- | --- 
Vitamins | A, B (complex), C, D, E, K
Minerals | Magnesium, Selenium, Copper, Iron, Zinc, Calcium
Antioxidants | Beta-carotene, Lutein, Lycopene, Co-Q10, Polyphenols, Curcumin (Turmeric), Acetyl-L-Carnitine
Other | Choline, Omega-3s, Ginko Biloba, Lion's Mane, Saffron

---


## Deficiency as Risk Factor
Very few supplements show direct effectiveness against dementia and Alzheimer's once symptoms begin.  However, there is strong evidence that ***deficiencies*** in many of the listed items increase the risk of developing dementia and Alzheimer's.

In these cases, supplementation is recommended as an effective form of prophylaxis.

Recommended supplements due to deficiency risk:  
Choline, Copper, Iron, Vitamin A, Vitamin B (complex), Vitamin C, Vitamin D, Vitamin K, Zinc

## Benefits by Supplement

| Supplement / Intervention | Result / Reference
| --- | ---:
Acetyl-L-Carnitine | Neuro-protective and helps during injury recovery[^11]. <br/> No evidence for direct cognitive performance improvement[^12].
Beta-carotene | Reduces risk of cognitive decline[^13]. <br/> Improved cognitive function (unclear if healthy adults)[^13].
Calcium | Neuro-protective (when combined with Vitamin D)[^2]. <br/> Note: Alzheimer's causes high serum levels[^14] [^15]. <br/> 
Choline | Benefits attention, memory, executive function in early stage Alzheimer's[^2]. <br/> Deficiency correlates with cognitive impairment[^26].
Co-Q10 | Higher serum levels associated with reduced risk of cognitive decline[^16]. <br/> Caveats for neurological effectiveness of supplementation[^17].
Copper | *Deficiency* (and excessive intake) increases cognitive decline risk[^2].
Curcumin | Potentially protective against decline[^2].<br/> Some evidence for improved memory[^2].
Ginko Biloba | No evidence for effectiveness against existing Alzheimer's[^1]. 
Iron | *Deficiency* increases cognitive decline risk[^2]. <br/> Supplementation positively impacts IQ[^2].
Lion's Mane | Delays neuronal death in neurodegenerative diseases, prevent memory loss (mouse model), improved cognitive function in mildly impaired[^24].
Lutein | Reduces risk of cognitive decline[^19]. 
Lycopene | Weak evidence for reduced risk of cognitive decline[^20] [^21].
Magnesium | Reduces risk of cognitive decline[^2].
Omega-3 | Effective in prevention and treatment of Alzheimer's[^2]. <br/> DHA may be more effective for prevention[^4].
Polyphenols <br/> (berries, tea, and cocoa) | Effective in reducing dementia/Alzhiemer's risk[^2]. <br/> Significant reduction in age-related memory and cognition decline[^25]. 
Saffron | Improvement in cognitive function for mild to severe AD[^24].
Selenium | Reduces risk of cognitive decline[^2].
Vitamin A | *Deficiency* causes cognitive impairment[^22] [^23].
Vitamin B (complex) | No evidence for effectiveness against existing Alzheimer's[^1]. <br/> *Deficiency* increases cognitive decline risk[^2].<br/> Improved memory and cognitive function[^2].
Vitamin C | *Deficiency* increases cognitive decline risk[^2].
Vitamin D | *Deficiency* increases cognitive decline risk[^2]. <br/> Improved mood and cognitive functions for 1500-4000 IU doses[^2].
Vitamin E | anti-Alzheimer’s[^1] <br/> Protects cell membranes, effective in reducing dementia/Alzhiemer's risk[^2].
Vitamin K | *Deficiency* increases cognitive decline risk[^2].
Zinc | *Deficiency* increases cognitive decline risk[^2]. 





[^1]: [Common Dietary Supplements for Cognitive Health](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3311304/)  
    Focused on dementia and Alzheimer's.  Mostly negative findings, but all specifically for effectiveness against already extand dementia and Alzheimer's.

[^2]: [Improving Cognitive Function with Nutritional Supplements in Aging: A Comprehensive Narrative Review of Clinical Studies Investigating the Effects of Vitamins, Minerals, Antioxidants, and Other Dietary Supplements](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC10746024/)  
    Very good and very recent, and with a table of specific recommendations.

[^4]: [Effects of Omega-3 Polyunsaturated Fatty Acids on Brain Functions: A Systematic Review](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC9641984/)
    - Very recent publication, 2022.
    - Improved learning, memory ability, cognitive well-being, and blood flow in the brain.
    - EPA supplementation shows higher cognitive performance (vs DHA supplementation).
    - DHA displays more neuro-protective qualities.

[^6]: [The Effect of Zinc Supplementation on Circulating Levels of Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor (BDNF)](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC9580557/)  
    Zinc supplementation is associated with higher serum BDNF. High levels of BDNF are associated with the improvement of cognitive and behavioral performance.
  
[^7]: [Ginkgo biloba extract improves cognitive function and increases neurogenesis by reducing Aβ pathology in 5xFAD mice](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8014356/)  
    mouse study, but shows dose dependence for ginko

[^11]: [L-Carnitine and acetyl-L-carnitine roles and neuroprotection in developing brain](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5621476/)
    - Interesting quote: *"Effective neuroprotection involves attenuating inflammation, preventing energy failure, preventing oxidative damage to key cellular and mitochondrial proteins, and maintaining neuronal and glial functions and biosynthetic capabilities."*
    - *"acetyl-L-carnitine fulfills these requirements and also has the additional capability to provide an acetyl-CoA that can be oxidized for energy"*

[^12]: [L‐carnitine for cognitive enhancement in people without cognitive impairment](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6464592/)  
    Meta-analysis showed no evidence for cognitive enhancement in healthy adults, but author's note that all studies (even those meeting minimum requirements for inclusion in the meta-analysis) were badly administered.

[^13]: [Carotenoids and Cognitive Outcomes: A Meta-Analysis of Randomized Intervention Trials](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7913239/)  
    Meta-analysis.  Shows strong preventative role in cognitive decline, and cognitive improvements with supplementation.

[^14]: [Serum calcium and cognitive function in old age](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17979900/)
    High serum calcium levels associated with worse cognitive performance and higher risk of cognitive decline.  Appears to be a symptom of Alzheimer's[^15].

[^15]: [Calcium Dysregulation in Alzheimer’s Disease: A Target for New Drug Development](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5713908/)
    Calcium channels become disregulated in Alzheimer's, resulting in high serum levels.

[^16]: [Coenzyme Q10 Levels Associated With Cognitive Functioning and Executive Function in Older Adults](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35908233/)
    Unclear whether supplementation is helpful, but high levels are correlated with positive outcomes.

[^17]: [Coenzyme Q10, Ageing and the Nervous System: An Overview](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8773271/)
    Endogenous production declines with age, but it is unclear whether exogenous Co-Q10 can cross the blood-brain barrier.  However, still a very good supplement for non-neurological reasons.

[^18]: [Lutein across the Lifespan: From Childhood Cognitive Performance to the Aging Eye and Brain](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6629295/)
    - After supplementation, verbal fluency scores improved significantly in the DHA-only, lutein-only, and combined treatment groups.
    - Memory scores and rate of learning improved significantly in the lutein + DHA group, who also displayed a trend toward more efficient learning.
    - Brain concentrations of lutein and DHA are a predictor of cognitive test scores measuring verbal fluency, working memory, and dementia in centenarians.

[^19]: [Lutein Has a Positive Impact on Brain Health in Healthy Older Adults: A Systematic Review of Randomized Controlled Trials and Cohort Studies](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8223987/)
    Meta-analysis.  Positive findings for learning, memory, etc.   Also may help prevent cognitive decline in aging.

[^20]: [Lycopene and cognitive function](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6558668/)
    Meta-analysis.  Weak positive findings for lycopene being protective against cognitive decline.

[^21]: [Lycopene attenuates western-diet-induced cognitive deficits via improving glycolipid metabolism dysfunction and inflammatory responses in gut–liver–brain axis](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41366-018-0277-9)
    Mouse model.  Protective effect from cognitive impairment. 

[^22]: [Vitamin A and Retinoic Acid in Cognition and Cognitive Disease](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32966186/)
    Deficiency causes cognitive impairment.  No effect on healthy adults.

[^23]: [Significance of vitamin A to brain function, behavior and learning](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3169332/)
    Deficiency causes cognitive impairment (sometimes permanent if it occurs during critical stages of development).

[^24]: [The effects of twenty-one nutrients and phytonutrients on cognitive function: A narrative review](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8445631/)
    Meta-analysis from 2021.

[^25]: [Effects of Nutrition on Cognitive Function in Adults with or without Cognitive Impairment: A Systematic Review of Randomized Controlled Clinical Trials](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8621754/)
    Meta-analysis from 2021.

[^26]: [Choline Intake Correlates with Cognitive Performance among Elder Adults in the United States](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8570899/)
    Deficiency correlates with low cognitive function.
